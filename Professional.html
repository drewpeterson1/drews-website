Bring it Around Town

Interested in the intersection of economics of the environment. I don't know why exactly I felt drawn to be the steward of the world. Maybe it is the good-guy sentiment that I grew up with. Interesting challenge to keep standard of living high while preserving the single planet we have. Was in Bali working on an organic rice farm, saw this dude coming to make hotel cards by melting plastic trash he found on the street. Got me thinking about what to with empty trash, realizing most of the container is exactly the same after use, just without the liquid. Try to sell it back to the companies or reuse the containers. Landed on a stream of thought seen here (excuse the Dana from Silicon Valley voice) about getting emtpty containers back around again. Joined a new startup doing that and learned a lot. Interesting that investing in a reusable container is worse for the environment if used once because more resources go into producing it, but if used dozens of times each use is better for the environment because rather than producing new containers, you just have to clean and ship a new one. It is an elegantly simple model to take waste and make it material, but it doesn't full capture logistics considerations, consumer interest, or cost considerations. Anyways, it taught me about the importance of convenience & affordability, and the mass market consumers. Also interesting that recycling is a business if you can get more money from selling the end material than it costs to collect & process it, otherwise businesses are gulted into subsidizing the disposal. Anyway, the intentional design of products can enable reuse and the full circle of Cradle to Cradle is interesting.




Something Ventured

An astonishing amount of the most successful companies start off as a few people who don't really know much of what they're doing. They're working on something they want to create. They don't have much money to create a full company yet. They need outsdie funding to build a team to work on this project. That is where Venture Capital comes in. Venture Capitalists back buckets of young companies with investments for portions of the company's equity. Some of these companies grow and mature into incredibly successful enterprises. The success from the winners more than covers the majority of cases where the investments melts to zero. What is fascinating are two things: (1) how close to the cutting edge of creation of new comapnies these investments are (2) how exponential the returns on just one winner can be (Power Law). In terms of investments, the public market is figured out -- put all you can into a low-cost Vanguard index fund. Don't try to beat the market. Don't pay someone to return below the market. Find success by reaping the solid market share of the returns of top corporations. On the private investing side, there is more to consider and evaluate -- more potential to drive and facilitate real value through investing in provate companies. Venture is exciting because it is at the intersection of new technology and understanding how to reap returns from well-placed investments.
